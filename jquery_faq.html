10 Bizarre and Interesting Facts About the U.S. National Parks

Yellowstone many not have been the first national park
Hot Springs National park in Arkansas was the first federally protected piece of land but it was not designated as a national park until five decades after Yellowstone.
The second US national park is now a state park
Mackinac National Park in Michigan was made a national park in 1875 and turned back into a state park in 1895.
The National Park Service was founded 44 years after Yellowstone became a national park.
The NPS was established in 1916 but the first declared national park was established in 1872.
The park ranger uniform was created by the U.S. Army

The National Park Service oversees more than 400 units.
Begining in 1933 the NPS was given the oversight of different types of lands including national monuments, historic sites, battlefields, lakeshores and recreation areas. Only 59 of these unit are actual national parks.
The US government loans land from the locals.
The NPS includes protected lands that are in US protectorate such as Guam, American Samoa, etc. The national park of America Samoa actually leases the land from local villages on the island. Every 50 years a new contract has to be negotiated.
The Carlsbad Caverns lunchroom debate continues.
The lunchroom within the caverns (750 ft underground) goes against the NPS code of keeping the parks natural and limitin in-park commercialism. However, Acts of Congress keep the cafeteria going and eviromentla studies have shown that the lunchroom poses no potential harm to the cave system.
Visitors were once encouraged to watch bears eat from dumpsters
In the early years the NPS did little to keep bears from obtaining human food. This led to issues of public safety and probelmatic bears. In one park they would feed dead horses to grizzlies so people could view them eat.
Theodore Roosevelt National Park was Teddy's mourning location
Roosevelt's mother and wife died on the same day, Valentine's Day in 1884. He went to the badlands of North Dakota were he once hunted bison as the felt the desolate landscape matched the condition of his heart and it was a place that he could begin to heal. That area later became the national park.
A Shenandoah National Park ranger was hit by lightning seven times in his career
Roy Sullivan was hit by lightning seven times and survived them all. He worked in the park between 1942 and 1977. He has stated that he was once struck by lightning as a child.

Yosemite National Park

  • Yosemite might be our nation’s 3rd national park, but it sparked the idea of national parks
  • Yosemite’s granite rock formations glow like fire at sunset
  • The park’s diverse landscape supports more than 400 species
  • Yosemite is home to one of the tallest waterfalls in the world

Yellowstone National Parks

  • Yellowstone encompasses 3,472 square miles (2,221,766 acres) which makes it larger than Rhode Island and Delaware combined
  • There are more than 10,000 hydrothermal features in Yellowstone. The four types of thermal features are geysers, hot springs, mud pots, and fumaroles
  • Yellowstone is home to more than 500 active geysers (more than half the world’s geysers)
  • Yellowstone Lake has 131.7 sq. miles of surface area and 141 miles of shoreline. It is the largest high elevation lake in North America

Glacier National Parks

  • Glacier National Park is a part of the world’s first international peace park
  • Glacier straddles the Continental Divide, allowing for extreme weather
  • Going-to-the-Sun Road is a spectacular marvel and a must see on your trip to Glacier
  • Evidence of human use in the area dates back over 12,000 years
